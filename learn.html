Overview

Most of our common energy sources, such as coal, oil, and petroleum, are nonrenewable and will eventually run out. There is a finite supply of them on our planet, and once we use them up, we won’t be able to make more. Because of this, the safer and more sustainable option is to transition to renewable energy.

Renewable (also called green or clean) energy is a source of energy that doesn’t run out. It harnesses the energy of natural processes such as the wind or the movement of water to generate electricity. Because we obtain it from infinite (or practically infinite) sources, we don’t have to worry about running out of energy.

Homeowners who are interested in using clean energy usually have two options - choose an electricity provider who uses mostly renewable energy sources, or install your own personal energy systems in your home. Both options are a good way to help the environment, and are often rewarded by government programs and the natural return on investment over time.

Solar

Solar energy is the most abundant form of renewable energy on Earth - in fact, the sunlight we receive in an hour and a half is enough to power our whole planet for a year. We can capture solar energy using either mirrors or photovoltaic (solar) panels. Solar panels are one of the cheapest clean home energy solutions, costing about $12,700 for the average homeowner and can last about 30 years.

Wind

Wind energy harnesses the kinetic energy of moving air to convert it into usable energy. Wind turbines are a common source of wind power, and can be used to power individual needs, such as homes, or can contribute to the entire energy grid. Although they’re a common sight in many inland areas, they can also be found at sea as part of offshore wind farms. Homeowners who install a small wind turbine on their property can expect to pay between $15,000 and $75,000.

Hydropower

Hydropower uses moving water to generate electricity. Facilities can range in size from huge dams built over wide rivers to small generators taking advantage of irrigation ditches. Some hydropower systems generate electricity without the use of a dam, instead channeling a portion of a stream through a powerhouse before allowing it to rejoin the main river. Hydropower relies on relatively stable rainfall and will be affected if water levels change - so it is more practical in some locations than in others. A personal hydroelectric system, known as microhydropower, typically costs between $15,000 - $55,000.

Geothermal

Geothermal energy comes from underground pockets of water that have been heated by the Earth’s internal energy. Wells bring up steam and hot water that we can use for electricity. Geothermal reservoirs are also used as heating and cooling systems for buildings, campuses, and communities, and can directly provide hot water for a variety of uses. A private geothermal system can cost anywhere from $8,000 to $24,000.

Marine

Marine, or ocean, energy harnesses the kinetic energy of waves and tides to generate electricity. In some cases, it can also harvest energy from differences in water temperatures through a process known as ocean thermal energy conversion. Common examples of marine energy systems include buoys (which take advantage of up/down motions of waves) and turbines (which take advantage of the currents and tides). Marine energy power plants are usually offshore, making them an impractical solution for an individual, but they are a good choice for many coastal communities.

Bioenergy

Bioenergy comes from organic plant- and algae-based matter that is converted into fuel or directly into energy. It is sourced from residential and commercial waste, as well as purpose-grown crops. You may already have a small bioenergy system in your home - a fireplace. However, if you plan to use bioenergy to heat your home, you should consider installing a biomass heating system for around $12,000.

Costs and Benefits

Changing infrastructure is never cheap or easy, and energy is no exception. Clean energy systems usually have a high initial cost, which turns many homeowners away. The cost is multiplied when a whole community is being considered, rather than a single building. It often seems simpler to keep the existing nonrenewable energy infrastructure.

However, when considering the costs associated with renewable energy, it’s important to also consider the benefits. Over time, many clean energy systems will provide a high return on investment. This means that the money they save on ongoing energy costs adds up to (and then exceeds) the money they could have saved by using a nonrenewable energy source. So, although renewable energy may seem expensive at first, over time, it becomes cheaper than the alternatives.

There are also many government-funded resources and programs that reward the use of clean energy, often in tax credits, but occasionally in loans or grants. These can help offset the cost of installing renewable energy systems, and are a win-win for you and for the environment.

Renewable Energy and the Environment

Emissions from nonrenewable energy sources such as coal have contributed to significant climate change - 2023 was the warmest year we have ever recorded. Current carbon dioxide levels are comparable to 4.1 million years ago, when sea levels were high enough to put most of the world’s major cities underwater, and the Arctic tundra was a forest. By 2050 (jin ust over 25 years), many major consequences of climate change will be irreversible, including the loss of the Amazon rainforest and major ocean systems.

We can significantly reduce our impact on the planet by switching to clean energy. Generating renewable energy, such as solar, wind, and hydroelectric, does not release carbon dioxide into the atmosphere - but nonrenewable energy production in the U.S. releases 1.65 billion metric tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere per year. Additionally, green energy typically results in less air and water pollution than combustible fuels, and often has a smaller physical footprint (size) than nonrenewable energy.
